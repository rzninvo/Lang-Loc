Language is enough most of the time.

LangLoc is a research project on language-based 3D indoor localisation. Given a written description of what someone sees, the system tries to guess where they are inside a known map of the building. No camera; no GPS; no QR codes. Just words.

Data & privacy

What we collect. The text you type, the photo it describes, how long you spent on each frame, and a randomly generated browser identifier so we can save your work between visits. We do not collect your name, email, IP address, location, or anything that identifies you personally. The browser identifier is a UUID stored in a cookie on your machine; clearing your cookies forgets you.

Where it goes. Your text is stored on a server at ETH Zürich. It is used as a query against the localisation system to compute aggregate metrics (median position error, recall at a threshold) for the research paper. We may release the anonymised text corpus together with the paper so other researchers can reproduce our results; if you do not want your text included in that release, close the tab now and don't write anything.

Deletion. If you'd like your descriptions removed, contact us with the rough date and approximate first sentence of one of your descriptions and we will delete the matching browser identifier's data. There is no public deletion endpoint; this is a small research site.

Why we need you

Most of the descriptions used to build the system were written by a language model. Reviewers of the paper rightly worried this introduces a closed-loop bias: the system learns to localise from text written by something that already knows the layout. To fix that, we need real people to describe the same images, in their own words, without seeing the map or the answers.

Your descriptions go into the same pipeline as the AI-written ones, and we measure the difference. That's the experiment.

What we ask for

Two to four sentences in first person, per photo. Around 25 words. Mention what's directly in front of you, what's on your left and right, and any objects that you'd notice immediately if you walked into the room. Don't try to be exhaustive; describe the few things that pin down the room.

How long it takes

About 10–15 minutes for a session of ten frames. You can stop whenever you like; everything is auto-saved. Coming back later in the same browser picks up where you left off, and you can revise anything you've written from the My descriptions page.

Who we are

Computer Vision Group, ETH Zürich and University of Zürich. Project lead: Roham Zendehdel Nobari (rzendehdel@ethz.ch). The site is open-source as part of the LangLoc research project.
