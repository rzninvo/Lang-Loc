How it works.

Three steps. You can stop any time and your work is auto-saved.

01

See a photo

We show you one indoor still at a time, taken from a real apartment scan. No two photos repeat for you.

02

Describe it in your own words

Two to four sentences in first person, the way you'd describe the room to someone who can't see it.

03

Move on

Your description goes into the localisation system as a query. We measure how well it can recover the camera position.

What helps.

No template, no right answer. These are just things that tend to help the system work.

1

Speak in first person.

Sentences like I'm standing, in front of me, on my left. The system uses position cues from your text to figure out where the camera is.

2

Mention how things relate.

A window above the desk, a chair next to the bookshelf. Spatial relations do most of the heavy lifting.

3

Pick distinctive things.

Walls, floors and ceilings are everywhere. A coffee maker, a teal shower curtain, a houseplant; things that make this room feel like itself.

4

Two to four sentences.

Around 25 words. A small bar fills as you type so you have a sense of how much is enough. If a photo is ambiguous, just describe what you can see and skip what you can't.

An example.

No template; this is just one natural way to describe what you see.

a natural description

I'm standing at the bathroom doorway, looking at the shower. A light blue curtain hangs across the shower stall in front of me. The walls are mosaic tiles in beige tones, and a small ceramic sink is to my far right.

Yours doesn't have to look like this. Any natural way of describing the room works as long as it's grounded in what you actually see.
